Mark Zuckerberg wears the same gray shirt every day. Now you can too.

By Kathy Li
July 19, 2017



Singularity in technology (especially when it concerns the hypothetical invention of artificial superintelligence possessing greater-than-human intelligence) may be a fearful concept to some, including sci-fi fanatics.

Singularity in clothing choices, however, seems to be amassing a growth in popularity within the startup community. Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg is one of the most eminent advocates of a minimalistic -- but not necessarily small -- wardrobe.


Screenshot via Mark Zuckerberg's Facebook profile

Zuckerberg believes that donning the same outfit to work every day enables him to focus his energy on more important decisions.

When asked about his staple outfits during his first-ever public Townhall Q&A in 2014, the then-31-year-old explained, “I really want to clear my life to make it so that I have to make as few decisions as possible about anything except how to best serve this community.”

“I'm in this really lucky position, where I get to wake up every day and help serve more than a billion people. And I feel like I'm not doing my job if I spend any of my energy on things that are silly or frivolous about my life,” He said.

Not all of us are in his same position yet, although we can certainly aspire to be. Klaus Buchroithner, CEO of Vresh, has started by making the famous “Z-shirt” slightly more accessible.

“I've been producing fashion all my life. [I’ve] always wanted to create something unique and special that people love, with great quality, sustainable and fair.”

Inspired by Zuckerberg’s idea and in hopes to follow shirt suit, Buchroithner began his comprehensive research on the Z-shirt. He found out that it’s made by Brunello Cucinelli, whom he thinks is an amazing fashion designer with a great hand for fabrics.

“Unfortunately you can't buy Mark's shirts in Brunello's store. So I decided to design and produce a replica that is as close to the original as possible,” Buchroithner shared.

“I was quite satisfied with the results and thought it would be quite cool to make them available to everyone who's interested. It definitely helped me to get a great start into the day and more important it really feels great when I wear it.”


Photo courtesy of Vresh CEO Klaus Buchroithner

At 40 euros apiece, some netizens have expressed thoughts of the Z-shirt being an overpriced offering. Others have pointed out -- in fairness -- that “fast food” clothing items’ cheap prices are often a result of workers being exposed to unethical sweatshop conditions.

Speaking of ethics, Buchroithner has also stated that 100% of the profits generated through shirt sales will be donated to the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative.

When all is said and done, the Z-shirt would probably make a great feel-good piece, both mentally and physically. ∎
